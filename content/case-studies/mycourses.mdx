<CsSection num="01" label="The Problem" title={<>One platform, shouting equally loud about <em>everything</em>.</>}>

MyCourses is the academic backbone of RIT — yet students hit stress, confusion, and friction when using it to manage assignments, deadlines, and collaboration. The interface gives a parking notice and a final-project deadline the same weight, so the things that matter slip through the cracks.

Rather than redesign the entire system, I focused on the moments that matter most: <em>checking what's due, submitting with confidence, and collaborating without leaving the platform.</em> When I mapped what students ran into, four problems kept surfacing:

<ProblemGrid>
<ProblemCard title="Cognitive overload">Information-dense interface with poor hierarchy created mental fatigue and made critical course materials hard to locate.</ProblemCard>
<ProblemCard title="Scattered assignments">Deadlines lived across email, Discord, printed handouts, and the portal — with no single source of truth.</ProblemCard>
<ProblemCard title="Poor deadline visibility">Upcoming deadlines lacked prominence, leading to missed submissions and last-minute scrambles.</ProblemCard>
<ProblemCard title="Limited collaboration">No way for students to connect with classmates or collaborate during studies and discussions.</ProblemCard>
</ProblemGrid>

<Caption>The four problems that framed the redesign — drawn from interviews and survey responses.</Caption>

</CsSection>

<CsSection num="02" label="The Research" title={<>Students weren't failing the work. They were failing to <em>trust the system</em>.</>}>

I ran a survey of 15 students alongside interviews and affinity mapping. Two findings reframed the whole project:

<StatRow>
<Stat value="27%">found it "very easy" to see upcoming assignment deadlines. The rest were guessing.</Stat>
<Stat value="60%">checked MyCourses multiple times just to confirm an assignment actually submitted — they didn't trust it to reassure them.</Stat>
</StatRow>

The pattern underneath both numbers was <strong>submission anxiety</strong> — a quiet, constant tax on every student. When I asked what frustrated them most about submitting, one answer dominated:

  title="What frustrates you most about submitting assignments?"

</CsSection>

<CsSection num="03" label="The Strategy" title={<>Design around <em>intent</em>, not features.</>}>

Instead of adding complexity, I structured the experience around what a student is actually trying to do when they open MyCourses. It's almost always one of three things — so every decision had to serve one of them with minimal friction.

<IntentGrid>
<Intent numeral="i." title="Check what's due">See the real workload at a glance — what's due, what's in progress, what's done.</Intent>
<Intent numeral="ii." title="Submit with confidence">Upload, preview, confirm — and never wonder whether it actually went through.</Intent>
<Intent numeral="iii." title="Coordinate with others">Discuss, share drafts, and give feedback without leaving the platform.</Intent>
</IntentGrid>

</CsSection>

<Problem title="One platform, shouting equally loud about everything.">

RIT's learning platform, MyCourses, is where students live during the semester — alongside email, Discord, printed handouts, and group chats. The interface gives a parking notice and a final-project deadline the same weight, so the things that matter slip through the cracks.

When I mapped what students were running into, four problems kept surfacing:

  src="/case-studies/mycourses/pulled-04.png"

<Evidence source="MyCourses student survey">
Submitting work feels more stressful than it should be.
</Evidence>

I scoped the work to the assignments-and-collaboration flow — the part that touches everyone, every week — rather than the entire LMS.

</Problem>

I interviewed RIT students across CS, Design, and Engineering, then ran a screener survey and built an affinity map from the responses to find the patterns worth designing against.

Three insights became the design's north star:

<Insight number="01" title="Notifications cause more anxiety than they resolve.">
  Students described phantom-buzzing — the platform treats every event as equally urgent, so none of them land.
  <Evidence source="MyCourses student survey">
    I keep checking MyCourses because I'm never sure if I submitted something correctly.
  </Evidence>
</Insight>

<Insight number="02" title="Students lean on classmates more than the platform.">
  When in doubt, "I'll check with a friend" beat opening MyCourses — collaboration was happening everywhere except inside the product.
  {/* TODO: an earlier draft cited a specific "4:1" ratio here — unverified, so stated qualitatively. Confirm with Palash. */}
</Insight>

<Insight number="03" title={'"Done" is invisible — there is no closure.'}>
  Submitting an assignment produced no real acknowledgement, so students re-checked compulsively to be sure it went through.
  <Evidence source="MyCourses student survey">
    I just want one place that tells me what's due and how behind I am.
  </Evidence>
</Insight>

</Research>

<Constraints title="What I committed to — and what I deliberately ignored.">

I framed the work as one question: **how might we turn anxious assignment-hunting into a calm, shared rhythm?** Three principles held me to it:

1. **Calm by default.** No surface should add anxiety; many should remove it.
2. **Make "done" feel done.** Closure matters more than features.
3. **Design for the friend group, not the lone student.**

The direction I deliberately killed was "filter-everything" — handing students granular notification controls. It tested poorly: people wanted *less* to think about, not more dials to tune. I caught it a wireframe later than I should have, which taught me to validate the scary flows earlier instead of the comfortable ones.

</Constraints>

<Solution title="One calm home, three jobs done.">

The redesign centers on a single dashboard that answers *what's due, what's done, and who can help* without switching tabs. Each surface maps back to a research insight.

<Outcome title="A second usability round — and what I'd do next.">

I ran a second usability round with students who hadn't seen earlier iterations, each completing three core tasks: submit an assignment, check on a group, and triage a busy week. Confidence in submission, time lost switching between tools, and perceived ease all moved in the right direction.

If I did it again, I'd validate the collaboration flow earlier. I shipped the calm-dashboard work first because it felt most urgent, but group sync was the harder design problem and I avoided it for a week because it felt scary. The lesson I carry forward: **research signal should outweigh a designer's gut about what feels "ready" to design.**

<Section number="02" label="The problem" title="Students drowning in notifications, missing the ones that matter.">

RIT runs on a 14-year-old learning platform called MyCourses. Students juggle it alongside email, Discord servers, printed handouts, and group chats. Assignments slip through the cracks — not because students aren't trying, but because the platform shouts equally loud about a parking notice and a final project deadline.

<UserQuote
  quote="I've missed three assignments this semester because notifications buried them under spam."
  attribution="Maya, 2nd-year CS"
/>

<PalashCharacter variant="thinking" size={120} />

The scope of the redesign was the **assignments + group sync** flow, not the entire LMS. I wanted to fix the part that affects everyone every week.

</Section>

<Section number="03" label="Research" title="What 12 interviews and 82 surveys actually told me.">

I ran 12 semi-structured interviews with current RIT students across CS, Design, and Engineering, plus an 82-respondent screener survey. I built an affinity map from quotes, then narrowed it to three insights that became the design's north star.

<Insight number="01" title="Notifications cause more anxiety than they solve">
  Students described phantom-buzzing every time MyCourses pinged them. The platform treats every event as equally urgent.
</Insight>

<Insight number="02" title="Students rely on classmates more than the platform">
  When in doubt, "I'll check with [friend]" beat opening MyCourses by a 4:1 ratio.
</Insight>

<Insight number="03" title='"Done" is invisible — no closure'>
  Submitting an assignment produced no celebration, no clear acknowledgement. Students re-checked compulsively.
</Insight>

<Section number="04" label="How might we" title="From insight to design problem.">

> How might we turn anxious assignment-hunting into a calm, shared rhythm?

I held myself to three principles through every design decision:

1. **Calm by default.** No surface should add anxiety; many should remove it.
2. **Make "done" feel done.** Closure matters more than features.
3. **Build for the friend group, not the individual.** Solo dashboards aren't how students actually work.

</Section>

<Section number="05" label="Process" title="Sketches, wireframes, and one design I killed.">

I sketched four directions on paper, took two to mid-fi, and tested both in a 5-person guerilla round. The "filter-everything" approach (give users granular notification controls) tested poorly — students wanted less to think about, not more dials to tune.

<Section number="07" label="Outcome" title="A second-round usability test, and what it showed.">

I ran a second usability round with 8 students who hadn't seen earlier iterations. Each completed three core tasks: submit an assignment, check on a group, and triage a busy week. Results:

<MetricCallout
  metrics={[
    { value: "2×", label: "Confidence in submission" },
    { value: "3×", label: "Less platform switching" },
    { value: "8/10", label: "SUS score, up from 5.1" }
  ]}
/>

Confidence was measured with a post-task 7-point Likert ("I'm sure my submission went through"). Platform-switching was self-reported in a follow-up survey.

</Section>

<Section number="08" label="Reflection" title="What I'd do differently — and what I carry forward.">

I shipped the notification redesign first because it felt the most urgent to me. In hindsight, the group-sync flow was the harder design problem and I should have validated it earlier — the killed mid-fi cost me a week.

What I carry forward: **research findings should hold more weight in roadmap order than designer intuition about what's "ready" to design**. I had clear signal that group sync was the more loaded problem, and I avoided it for a week because it felt scary.

</Section>
